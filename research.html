CLIENT — LEGACY CONFECTIONERY BRAND

"Modern Love" — Transmedia Anxiety

Qual

The problem — A legacy confectionery brand wanted its biggest gifting occasion to reflect what love actually feels like today.

The solution — Ran 9 extended mini-groups and 6 couple depth interviews across 5 relationship-stage cohorts, using a projective technique to surface anxieties a standard focus group wouldn't reach.

The impact — Uncovered "transmedia anxiety" as a defining feature of modern intimacy — directly shifting the Valentine's strategy from grand gestures to small, relatable moments.

My contribution — Designed the recruitment questionnaire and screening criteria per cohort, ran backchecks on high-stakes units, and proposed the projective exercise that became the study's key unlock. Analyzed the resulting data to map how anxiety showed up differently for men and women across each relationship stage, turning those patterns into stage-specific roadmaps that fed directly into positioning strategy.

CLIENT — GLOBAL CONFECTIONERY MAJOR

Atlas — Mapping the Indian Chocolate Palate

Qual

The problem — A global confectionery major needed to define its innovation roadmap by mapping how Indian consumers experience chocolate — a category with no unified sensory language.

The solution — Ran extended product-testing groups across 5 eat-states, benchmarking imported chocolates against existing variants and applying a Kano model to rank preferences.

The impact — Findings became a full NPD brief — pip size, roast quality, melt harmony — translating raw consumer vocabulary into product specification.

My contribution — Created the visual need-state mood boards used in sessions and helped build the data collation structure across this large multi-city study. Built decks for senior leadership explaining how 2 of the 5 eat-states were delineated, and participated in brainstorm sessions on how the palate's perception of new confectionery formats could shape the brand's innovation direction.

CLIENT — LEGACY CONFECTIONERY BRAND

Gems — Designing Research for Two Audiences at Once

Qual

The problem — A legacy confectionery brand needed to concept- and product-test two different new launches, evaluated across two very different stakeholders at once — the kids who'd actually eat the product, and the moms who'd actually buy it.

The solution — Designed and ran a dual-audience study: pre-focus-group surveys with kids walked through product testing and completed via unbiased questionnaires, paired with facilitated, probing sessions with moms to surface purchase-side concerns — run side by side rather than as two disconnected studies.

The impact — Reconciled what excited kids with what reassured mothers into a single family-appeal strategy for both launches, rather than optimizing for one audience at the expense of the other.

My contribution — Facilitated and probed directly with the kid respondents — administering the pre-group surveys and product walkthroughs in an unbiased way — while adapting moderation style across two very different stakeholder groups in the same study, drawing on empathy and sensitivity to each audience's needs.

CLIENT — LEGACY WELLNESS BRAND

The Autonomy / Wellness Paradox

Qual

The problem — A legacy wellness brand wanted to understand the modern Indian woman's autonomy and health needs across generations, and test a new brand concept built on that tension.

The solution — Ran qualitative depths and concept testing across multiple Indian markets to surface unmet needs and understand the tensions and pain points shaping women's attitudes toward sexual health, wellness, and autonomy today. Concept- and name-tested the new brand for fit and resonance across those markets, surfacing a central paradox along the way: as women gained autonomy, their reproductive health quietly declined.

The impact — One tested name ("i choose") was rejected by the target segment as arrogant rather than confident — flagged before further investment, reshaping the brand territory to autonomy and wellness.

My contribution — Designed the discussion guide for the focus groups, ran quality checks on moderation, and analyzed focus group data across markets to help shape brand strategy. Flagged the naming risk directly from the data and helped reframe the positioning recommendation that followed.

CLIENT — LEGACY INDIAN FASHION RETAILER

Decoding Fashion Confidence — Attitude-Based Segmentation

QualQuant

The problem — A legacy Indian fashion retailer needed to understand fashion attitudes and behaviors across a genuinely diverse Indian audience — specifically, how confidence and interest in fashion intersect and diverge, and how that shapes real retail choice, across both strong and weaker-performing markets.

The solution — Ran immersions and shop-along studies across multiple Indian cities, from heritage-driven markets like Lucknow to more fashion-forward urban centers, building nuanced consumer personas (e.g., "Aspirants" vs. "Fashion-Forward Aspirants") grounded in local cultural context. Mapped the actual shopper journey through store layout market by market, comparing how in-depth interview narratives and real shop-along behavior aligned or diverged — and how that relationship itself shifted between strong and weak markets. Paired the qualitative work with quantitative analysis of a fashion-attitude battery to build a statistically grounded attitude-based segmentation model.

The impact — Segmentation, market-level nuance, and journey mapping directly shaped retail recommendations — assortment gaps, display and ambience improvements, and where the brand was losing share to competitors on trend relevance and variety, with clarity on which fixes mattered most in which type of market.

My contribution — Ran recruitment and backchecks for the groups, and oversaw the shop-along component — tracking strong points and pain points in the real shopper journey and comparing how those journeys differed across markets and against what respondents said in their interviews. Also built and ran a multi-day digital diary panel — a "fashion journal" tracking personal style, wardrobe, and shopping behavior over a week — then synthesized that diary data together with the interview findings into visual, market-specific decks for the client.
